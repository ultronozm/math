Gan, Triality and the Spin \(L\)-function of \(\mathrm{PGSp}(6)\)

There are two parts. The global part is joint with G. Chenevier. The local part is joint with G. Savin.

Let’s start by recalling the rough statement of Langlands functoriality. Let’s say we have two groups \(G\) and \(H\), over a local or global field \(F\). Let’s suppose that we have a homomorphism of Langlands dual groups \[f : G^\vee (\mathbb{C} ) \rightarrow H^\vee (\mathbb{C} ).\] Then, associated to this map, one is supposed to get a lifting

  • in the local setting, given by \[f _\ast : \mathop{\mathrm{Irr}}G \rightarrow \mathop{\mathrm{Irr}}H\] (perhaps up to \(L\)-packets), and

  • in the global setting, by \[f _\ast : \mathcal{A} (G) / \sim \rightarrow \mathcal{A} (H) / \sim\] between isomorphism classes of automorphic representations, where \(\sim\) denotes the equivalence relation of near equivalence, which means that, given \(\pi = \otimes_v \pi_v\) and \(\pi ' = \otimes_v \pi_v '\), we have \(\pi \sim \pi '\) if for almost all places \(v\), we have \(\pi_v \cong \pi_v '\).

These should satisfy the compatibility that, in the global setting, for \(\pi \in \mathcal{A}(G)\), we should have \[f _\ast (s (\pi_v )) = s (f _\ast (\pi)_v ),\] where \(s (\pi_v ) \in G^\vee / \mathrm{conj}\) is the Satake parameter of \(\pi_v\). We then call \(f _\ast\) a weak lifting.

Example 1.

  1. Adjoint (Gelbart–Jacquet). \[f = \underbrace \mathop{\mathrm{Ad}} : {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_2 \rightarrow {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_3,\] which we can also think of as \(\det^{-1} \otimes {\mathop{\mathrm{Sym}}}^2\).

  2. Rankin–Selberg. Ramakrishnan: \[f = \boxtimes : {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_2 \times {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_2 \rightarrow {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_4 .\] Kim–Shahidi: \[{\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_2 \times {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_3 \rightarrow {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_6,\] which they used to establish functoriality of the symmetric cube.

  3. Arthur: \[f = \mathop{\mathrm{std}}: G^\vee \rightarrow {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_N ,\] where \(G^\vee\) is one of \({\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_{2 n}\), \({\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_{2 n + 1}\), \({\mathop{\mathrm{Sp}}}_{2 n}\). When the representation on the source group is generic, this was proved earlier by CKPSS using converse theorems; those methods were later extended to all cuspidal representations by Cai–Friedberg–Kaplan

Today we’d like to talk about three new cases of weak lifting.

  1. \(\mathop{\mathrm{Ad}}: {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_3 \rightarrow {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_8\) (here the notation refers to the dual side, at the level of complex points).

  2. \({\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_2 \times {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_4 \rightarrow {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_8\).

  3. \({\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_7 = {\mathop{\mathrm{PGSp}}}_6^\vee \xrightarrow{\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}} {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_8\).

  1. These are proved under the following conditions. cuspidal \(\pi\) of \({\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_3\) with a certain local property: \(\pi_{v_0 }\) is a discrete series representation for some place \(v_0\) (necessarily a finite place, since there are no discrete series for \({\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_3\) at an archimedean place).

  2. For cuspidal representations \(\pi \otimes \sigma\) of \({\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_2 \times {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_4\) of symplectic type, i.e., the image of the Galois representations should factor through a symplectic group. For \({\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_2\), this just means that the central character is trivial. For \({\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_4\), it means that the \(L\)-parameter factors through \({\mathop{\mathrm{Sp}}}_4\). So if you’d like, you can think of \(\pi \otimes \sigma\) as a representation of \({\mathop{\mathrm{PGL}}}_2 \times {\mathop{\mathrm{PGSp}}}_4\), or if you’d like, of \({\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_3 \times {\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_5\).

  3. For cuspidal \(\pi\) on \({\mathop{\mathrm{PGSp}}}_6\) such that \(\pi |_{{\mathop{\mathrm{Sp}}}_6}\) has generic \(A\)-parameter (basically, the tempered case).

What is common between these? They all land in \({\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_8\): in each case, we have a map \[f : G^\vee \rightarrow {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_8.\] But the maps factor through smaller maps. For example, all of these maps factor through the map \[\mathop{\mathrm{std}}: {\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_8 \rightarrow {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_8.\] The group \({\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_8\) is not simply-connected, but has a simply-connected \(2\)-fold cover \[p : {\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_8 \rightarrow {\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_8.\] It turns out that we can lift \(f\) further to a map \[i : G^\vee \rightarrow {\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_8.\] Thus we can perform the lifting \(f_*\) associated to \(f\) in three steps: \[f _\ast = {\mathop{\mathrm{std}}}_\ast \circ p _\ast \circ i _\ast.\] Observe that:

  • \({\mathop{\mathrm{std}}}_\ast = \mathrm{Arthur}\).

  • \(p\) is dual to \[p^\vee : {\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_8 \rightarrow \mathrm{PGSO}_8.\] Then \(p _\ast\) is just pullback of automorphic forms along \(p\): \[p _\ast = (p^\vee )^\ast.\]

  • It remains to construct \(i _\ast\). To that end, let’s try to understand the map \(i\) better. Let’s illustrate with the case \(G^\vee = {\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_7\), which is perhaps least familiar. We have \({\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_7 \hookrightarrow {\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_8\). The cover \({\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_8 \rightarrow {\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_8\) pulls back to the cover \({\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_7 \rightarrow {\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_7\), giving a map \[\begin{CD} {\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_7 @> j>> {\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_8 \\ @VVV @VVV \\ {\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_7 @>>> {\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_8.\\ \end{CD}\] Is this \(j\) the same as the \(i\) above? No. The composition \(p \circ j\) is reducible, splitting up \(8 = 7 + 1\). On the other hand, the composition \(p \circ i\) is the irreducible “Spin representation”. We will see, however, that \(i\) and \(j\) are related by triality.

What is triality? If you have some group \(G\) over \(F\), then we have this short exact sequence \[\operatorname{Inn} (G) \rightarrow \mathop{\mathrm{Aut}}(G) \rightarrow \mathrm{Out} (G).\] We know also that at least for \(G\) simply-connected or adjoint, the group \(\mathrm{Out}(G)\) consists of automorphisms of the Dynkin diagram \(\mathrm{Dyn} (G)\): \[\mathrm{Out} (G) = \mathop{\mathrm{Aut}}(\mathrm{Dyn} (G)).\] In the case of interest with \(D_8\), the Dynkin diagram is \(D_4\): \[\begin{tikzpicture} \node[draw,circle,fill=black,inner sep=2pt] (a) at (0,0) {}; \node[draw,circle,fill=black,inner sep=2pt] (b) at (1,0) {}; \node[draw,circle,fill=black,inner sep=2pt] (c) at (2,0) {}; \node[draw,circle,fill=black,inner sep=2pt] (d) at (1,1) {}; \draw (a) -- (b) -- (c); \draw (b) -- (d); \end{tikzpicture}\] The outer automorphism group is thus isomorphic to the symmetric group \(S_3\) (corresponding to permutations of the vertices of the above diagram that fix the central vertex). Let \(\theta \in \S_3\) be an element of order \(3\). Fact: \[\begin{CD} {\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_7 @> j>> {\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_8 @> \theta >> {\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_8 \\ @VVV @VVpV @VVpV \\ {\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_7 @>>> {\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_8 @. {\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_8.\\ \end{CD}\] Then \[p \circ \theta \circ j\] is the Spin representation of \({\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_7\). In other words, we have \[\theta \circ j = i.\] From our \(\theta \in \mathop{\mathrm{Aut}}(\mathrm{Spin}_8 )\), we get \(\theta^\vee \in \mathop{\mathrm{Aut}}(\mathrm{PGSO}_8)\). We get the following diagram:

\[\begin{tikzpicture}[descr/.style={fill=white,inner sep=2.5pt}] \matrix (m) [matrix of math nodes, row sep=3em, column sep=2em] { {\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_8 & G^\vee & & {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_8 \\ & {\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_8 & {\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_8 & {\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_8 \\ }; \path[->,font=\scriptsize] (m-1-1) edge node[above] {$=$} (m-1-2) (m-1-2) edge node[right] {$j$} (m-2-2) edge node[below right] {$i$} (m-2-3) (m-2-2) edge node[below] {$\theta$} (m-2-3) (m-2-3) edge node[below] {$p$} (m-2-4) (m-2-4) edge node[right] {} (m-1-4); \end{tikzpicture}\]

The functoriality \(\theta _\ast\) is pullback by \(\theta^\vee\), i.e., we have \[f^{\theta^\vee } (g) = f ( {\theta^\vee }^{-1}(g) ).\] We get \[\overline{j} _\ast : \mathcal{A} ({\mathop{\mathrm{Sp}}}_6 ) \rightarrow \mathcal{A} ({\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_8).\] This is given by the classical theta correspondence, say \(\Theta\). We are looking at a map that covers it. We get that \[j _\ast : \mathcal{A} ({\mathop{\mathrm{PGSp}}}_6 ) \rightarrow \mathcal{A} (\mathrm{PGSO}_8 )\] is given by similitude \(\Theta\).

How about some of the other cases, such as that of Rankin–Selberg? We still get a diagram \[\begin{CD} {\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_3 \times {\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_5 @>j>> {\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_8 \\ @VVV @VVV \\ {\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_3 \times {\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_5 @>>\bar{j}> {\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_8. \\ \end{CD}\] So we get that \(p \circ j\) is \(3 + 5\), while \(p \circ i\) is \(2 \times 4\). But as before, they are related via the outer automorphism: we have \(\theta \circ j = i\).

For the remaining case, we are looking at \(\mathop{\mathrm{Ad}}: {\mathop{\mathrm{PGL}}}_3 \rightarrow {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_8\), \(p : {\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_8 \rightarrow {\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_8\) and \(\tilde{\mathop{\mathrm{Ad}}} : {\mathop{\mathrm{PGL}}}_3 \rightarrow {\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_8\).

Fact: there are two different splittings over \(A_3\), given by \(\theta_1\) and \(\theta_2\). We have \(A_3 \triangleleft S_3 = \mathrm{Out}(G)\) which lifts to \(A_3 \rightarrow \mathop{\mathrm{Aut}}(G)\). We have \[{\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_8^{\theta_1 } \cong G_2,\] \[{\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_8^{\theta_2 } \cong {\mathop{\mathrm{PGL}}}_3.\] Apply the theory of twisted endoscopy to \((\mathrm{PGSO}_8, \theta )\), in particular, the stable twisted trace formula (Moeglin–Waldspurger). The twisted endoscopic groups are \(G_2\), \({\mathop{\mathrm{SL}}}_3\) and \({\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_4\). We need \[I^{(\mathrm{PGSO}_8, \theta )}(f) = c_1 \cdot S^{G_2 } (f^{G_2 }) + c_2 S^{{\mathop{\mathrm{SL}}}_3 } (f^{{\mathop{\mathrm{SL}}}_3 }) + c_3 S^{{\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_4 } (f^{{\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_4 }),\] \[f \in C_c^\infty (\mathrm{PGSO}_8 (\mathbb{A} )).\]

Let’s now discuss a local variant of the spin lifting, joint with G. Savin. We would like to construct a map (local, say in the non-archimedean case) \[{\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_\ast : \mathop{\mathrm{Irr}}{\mathop{\mathrm{PGSp}}}_6 (F) \rightarrow \mathop{\mathrm{Irr}}{\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_8 (F).\] First, there’s a theorem called theta dichotomy, which allows us to split \[\begin{CD} \mathop{\mathrm{Irr}}{\mathop{\mathrm{PGSp}}}_6 (F) = {\mathop{\mathrm{Irr}}}^- {\mathop{\mathrm{PGSp}}}_6 (F) \sqcup {\mathop{\mathrm{Irr}}}^+ {\mathop{\mathrm{PGSp}}}_6 (F) @> \Theta >> \mathop{\mathrm{Irr}}\mathrm{PGSO}_8 \\ @V {\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_\ast VV @V \theta = \mathrm{triality} VV \\ \mathop{\mathrm{Irr}}{\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_8 (F) @. \mathop{\mathrm{Irr}}\mathrm{PGSO}_8 \\ @VV \mathrm{Art} V @V (p^\vee )^+ VV \\ \mathop{\mathrm{Irr}}{\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_8 (F) @= \mathop{\mathrm{Irr}}{\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_8 (F). \\ \end{CD}\] On the other hand, we have a map \[\Theta : \mathop{\mathrm{Irr}}{\mathop{\mathrm{PGSp}}}_6(F) \rightarrow \mathop{\mathrm{Irr}}\mathrm{PGO}_{5,1},\] which we can then restrict to \[\mathop{\mathrm{Irr}}\mathrm{PGSO}_{5, 1} / \mathrm{PGO}_{5,1},\] which is the same as \[\mathop{\mathrm{Irr}}{\mathop{\mathrm{PGL}}}_2(D) / \mathrm{outer}\] (here \(D\) is a quaternion division algebra over \(F\)), which we can then lift in the spirit of Jacquet–Langlands to \[\mathop{\mathrm{Irr}}{\mathop{\mathrm{PGL}}}_4 (F) / \mathrm{outer},\] which we can then isobaric sum back to \(\mathop{\mathrm{Irr}}{\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_8(F)\), i.e., \[\{\pi, \pi^\vee \} \mapsto \pi \boxplus \pi^\vee = {\mathop{\mathrm{Ind}}}_P^{{\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_8 } \pi \times \pi^\vee.\]

How about some applications? Well, we get the theory of \(\gamma\)-factors for \(\mathrm{PGSO}_8 \times {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_r\) with respect to \[\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}\times \mathop{\mathrm{std}}: {\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_8 (\mathbb{C} ) \times {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_r (\mathbb{C} ) \xrightarrow{\boxtimes } {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_8 (\mathbb{C} ) \times {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_r (\mathbb{C}).\]

Definition 2. We set \[\gamma (s, \pi \otimes \sigma, \mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}\times \mathop{\mathrm{std}}, \psi ) := \gamma (s, {\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_\ast (\pi) \times \sigma, \psi ),\] where the right hand side is the Rankin–Selberg \(\gamma\)-factor.

Theorem 3. This system of invariants has the following properties:

  • Unramified property.

  • Multiplicativity: compatibility with parabolic induction. (This reduces the determination of this system of invariants to the case of supercuspidals.)

  • Restricted global property: if you have a cuspidal representation, then from these \(\gamma\)-factors, you can define local \(L\)-factors and \(\varepsilon\)-factors so that the expected functional equation holds, but with the restriction that it holds only for some cuspidal representations.

Remark 4. These properties characterize this system of invariants, even with the restriction on the global property.

As a further application, we get a weak LLC for \({\mathop{\mathrm{PGSp}}}_6\). Usually one expects this to be a map \[\mathrm{LLC} : \mathop{\mathrm{Irr}}{\mathop{\mathrm{PGSp}}}_6 \twoheadrightarrow \Phi ({\mathop{\mathrm{PGSp}}}_6 ) = \left\{ \phi : \mathrm{WD}_F \rightarrow {\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_7 (\mathbb{C} ) \right\} / \sim.\] We can compose with \[\Phi ({\mathop{\mathrm{PGSp}}}_6 ) \twoheadrightarrow \Phi_w ({\mathop{\mathrm{PGSp}}}_6 ) := \operatorname{image} \left( \Phi ({\mathop{\mathrm{PGSp}}}_6 ) \xrightarrow{\mathop{\mathrm{std}}\times \mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}} \Phi ({\mathop{\mathrm{Sp}}}_6 ) \times \Phi ({\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_8)\right).\] \({\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}}_7\) is not “acceptable” in the sense of Michael Larsen, which has the consequence that this last map is not injective. But if we’re assessing these \(L\)-parameters via \(\mathop{\mathrm{std}}\) or \(\mathop{\mathrm{Spin}}\), then this is sufficient.

Mathilde, Counting non-tempered automorphic forms using endoscopy

Joint work with Rahul Dalal.

Let \(F\) be a number field,w ith adele ring \(\mathbb{A}\) and associated notation \(F_v, q_v, \mathcal{O}_F\).

For \(G\) reductive over \(F\), we get \[G (\mathbb{A} ) \circlearrowright L^2_{\mathop{\mathrm{disc}}} \left( G (F) \backslash G (\mathbb{A} ) \right) = \oplus_\pi m (\pi) \pi, \quad \pi = \pi _\infty \otimes \pi_f.\]

  • \(\pi_0\): specific representation at \(\infty\),

  • \(\mathfrak{n}\): ideal of \(\mathcal{O}_F\),

  • \(K (\mathfrak{n} ) \subset G (\mathbb{A}_f)\): principal level congruence subgroup,

Goal. Quantitative description of \[m (\pi_0, \mathfrak{n} ) = \sum_{ \substack{ \pi : \\ \pi _\infty = \pi_0 } } m (\pi) \dim \pi_f^{K (\mathfrak{n} )}.\]

Question 1. How does \(m (\pi_0 , \mathfrak{n} )\) grow as \(\lvert \mathfrak{n} \rvert \rightarrow \infty\)?

On a related note:

Question 2. Set \(\Gamma (\mathfrak{n} ) = G(F) \cap K(\mathfrak{n})\), \(X (\mathfrak{n} ) = \Gamma (\mathfrak{n} ) \backslash G _\infty / K _\infty\). How does \(\dim H_{(2)}^i (X (\mathfrak{n}), \mathbb{C} )\) grow as \(\mathfrak{n} \rightarrow \infty\)? Reduces to main question via Matsushima’s formula by taking \(\pi_0\) cohomological.

de George–Wallach (’78): \(G\) semisimple, \(\Gamma (\mathfrak{n} )\) compact: \[\lim_{\lvert \mathfrak{n} \rvert \rightarrow \infty } \frac{m (\pi_0, \mathfrak{n} )}{\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}(X (\mathfrak{n} ))} = \begin{cases} d (\pi_0 ) & \pi_0 : \text{discrete series}, \\ 0 & \text{ otherwise.} \end{cases}\] We have \[\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}(X (\mathfrak{n} )) \asymp [K (1) : K (\mathfrak{n} )].\]

Many results since: Delorme, Clozel, Savin, Finis–Lapid–Mueller.

For discrete series: Shin–Templier (’16):

  • Asymptotic with error term in both the weight and level aspect.

  • Sato–Tate in families.

Question 3. For \(\pi_0\) non-tempered, can we find \(\delta (\pi_0 )\) such that \[m (\pi_0 , \mathfrak{n} ) \asymp \mathop{\mathrm{vol}}(X (\mathfrak{n} ))^{\delta (\pi_0)}?\]

If we assume Ramanujan, then there are no non-tempered representations. However, motivated by the idea that Ramanujan should be asymptotically false.

Conjecture 1 (Sarnak–Xue). \(m (\pi_0, \mathfrak{n} ) \ll_\varepsilon\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}(X (\mathfrak{n} ))^{2 / p (\pi_0 ) + \varepsilon}\), where \[p (\pi_0 ) := \inf \left\{ p \geq 2 : \pi_0 \hookrightarrow L^p ( G _\infty ) \right\}.\]

Blomer–Assing, Jana–Kamber, Marshall–Shin.

Our result first concerns general cohomological representations.

Theorem 5 (Dalal–GG). Let \(E/F\) be a CM field. Let \(G_{/F}\) be unitary and defined with respect to \(E\). Let \(\pi_0\) be cohomological, with \(\mathfrak{n}\) split in \(E\).

  1. There exists \(R (\pi_0)\), explicitly computable and small enough so as to be nontrivial, such that \[m (\pi_0, \mathfrak{n} ) \ll_\varepsilon\lvert \mathfrak{n} \rvert^{R (\pi_0 ) + \varepsilon}.\]

  2. If, additionally, \(E/F\) is unramified, and \(\pi_0\) is “odd generalized Sato–Kurokawa maxed”, then we can get a weighted count: for any finite place \(v \nmid \mathfrak{n}\), and any \(f_v \in \mathcal{H}_v^{\mathrm{unr}} (G_v)\), we have \[\begin{gathered} \lvert \mathfrak{n} \rvert^{- R (\pi_0 )} L_{\pi_0 } (\mathfrak{n} )^{-1} \sum_{ \substack{ \pi : \\ \pi_0 = \pi _\infty } } m (\pi) \dim \pi_f^{K (\pi) } \mathop{\mathrm{trace}}\pi_v (f_v ) = M (\pi_0 ) \mu_r^{\operatorname{Planch}(\pi_0 )} (\tilde{f}_v ) \\ + \operatorname{O}\left( \lvert \mathfrak{n} \rvert^{- A} q_v^{B + C (f_v )} \right) \end{gathered}\] for \(\mathfrak{n}\) large enough and for some (non-explicit) constants \(A,B,C\).

Some consequences:

  • We do better than the conjecture of Sarnak–Xue for cohomological \(\pi_0\) at split level. We can do it for every representation of every unitary group except a certain \(\mathop{\mathrm{U}}(2,2)\) representation that Marshall also couldn’t address in his paper. We conjecture some sharp upper bounds.

  • Applications to cohomology: if \(\Gamma (\mathfrak{n} ) \subset \mathop{\mathrm{U}}(N - a, a ) \times K\), then there exists \(R (i, N, a)\) such that \[\dim H^i (X (\mathfrak{n} ), \mathbb{C} ) \ll_\varepsilon\lvert \mathfrak{n} \rvert^{R (N, i, a)}, \quad i < \mathrm{middle}.\] For example, we recover Marshall–Shin’s bound for \(\mathop{\mathrm{U}}(N - 1, 1)\) of \(R (N, i, a) = N_i\). Lower bounds: we show that Marshall–Shin is sharp if \(i \equiv N-1 \pod{2}\).

We also get a Sato–Tate result in families. Assuming

  • \(\pi_0\): oGSKm.

  • Sample Satake parameters coming from places \(v \nmid \mathfrak{n}\), all having the same splitting behavior in \(E\).

  • \(\lvert \mathfrak{n} \rvert \rightarrow \infty\) much faster than \(\prod_v q_v\).

We get then that Satake parameters of \(\pi = \pi _\infty \otimes \pi_f\) equidistribute on average with respect to \(\mu_{\mathrm{ST}}^{ (\pi_0 )}\), the Sato–Tate pushed forward from \(G (T_0)\).

The theorem is conditional on the endoscopic classification for unitary groups, but perhaps this will be addressed even in a talk this week.

That’s it for the applications. Now, what are the main tools for the proof?

The main input is the endoscopic classification of representations (Arthur, Mok, Kaletha–Minguez–Shin–White). We’ll give only the coarsest outline, to explain how we use it. \[L^2_{\mathop{\mathrm{disc}}} (G (F) \backslash G (\mathbb{A} )) = \bigoplus_\psi \bigoplus_{\pi \in \Pi_\psi (\varepsilon_\psi )} \pi.\] We can write \[\psi = \boxplus_i \tau_i [d_i ],\] where \(\tau_i\) is a self-dual cuspidal on \({\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_{N_i }\) and \(d_i\) is the dimension of a representation of the Arthur \({\mathop{\mathrm{SL}}}_2\). Here \[\psi : L_F \times {\mathop{\mathrm{SL}}}_2 \rightarrow {}^L G,\] \[\psi = \bigoplus_i \varphi_i \otimes v (d_i ).\] Endoscopic decomposition mirrors the decomposition of the full spectrum of \({\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_N\), built by from parabolic induction from cuspidals (Moeglin–Waldspurger). Here \(\Pi_\psi\) is an \(A\)-packet, while \(\varepsilon_\psi\) encodes the arithmetic information.

Definition 6. A shape of rank \(N\) is going to be a sequence of numbers \[\Delta = (N_i, d_i, \lambda_i )_i ,\] where \(\lambda\) is a regular integral infinitesimal character of rank \(N_i\), such that \[\sum_i N_i d_i = N.\]

Example 7. \(\Sigma_\lambda := (N, 1, \lambda)\).

Example 8. \(\mathrm{oGSK} := (N_1, 1, \lambda_1 ) (1 , d_2, \lambda_2 ) \dotsb (1, d_r, \lambda_r )\), where the \(d_i\) are odd and distinct.

\(\Delta\):

  • determines \(\psi _\infty\)

  • right data for induction

  • \(\pi_0\) gives rise to finitely many shapes.

Remark 9. When we have these Saito–Kurokawa shapes, we conjecture that the correct bound should be \[R(\chi) = \frac{1}{2} \left( N^2 + \sum_i N_i (N_i - d_i^2 + 1) \right).\]

Step 0: \[\sum_{\psi \in \Delta } \sum_{ \substack{ \pi \in \Pi_\psi (\varepsilon) \\ \pi _\infty = \pi_0 } } \dim \pi_f^{K (\mathfrak{n} )} = m_\Delta (\pi_0, \mathfrak{n} ) = I_\Delta^G (f_0 f (\mathfrak{n} )).\]<++> Step 1: stabilizer. \[I_\Delta^G (f_0 f (\mathfrak{n} )) = S^G (\mathrm{EP}_\lambda f (\mathfrak{n} ) ) + \sum_{\mathcal{H} } (- 1 )^{\mathcal{H} } I_{\Delta}^{\mathcal{H} } \left( \mathrm{EP}_\lambda^{\mathcal{H} } f (\mathfrak{n} )^{\mathcal{H} } \right).\] Here the sum over \(\mathcal{H}\) is of lower order in \(\lvert \mathfrak{n} \rvert\).

Next, study \(S_\Delta^G\). For \(\Delta = (N_i, d_i, \lambda_i )_i\), the dream is to show that \[S_\Delta^G (\mathrm{EP}_\lambda (f (\mathfrak{n} ))) = \prod_i \left( S_{\Sigma_{\lambda_i }}^{G_i '} \left( \mathrm{EP}_{\lambda_i } (f (\mathfrak{n} ))^{G_i '} \right)^{d_i } \right).\]

Step 3: compute \(S_{\Sigma_\lambda }^{G}\). We can compute \(S^G (\mathrm{EP}_\lambda (f (\mathfrak{n} )))\) (Shin–Templier). On the other hand, we can write it as \[S_{\Sigma_\lambda }^G (\mathrm{EP}_\lambda (f (\mathfrak{n} ))) + \sum_{ \substack{ \Delta \neq \Sigma_\lambda : \\ \inf (\Delta ) = \lambda } } S_\Delta^G (\mathrm{EP}_\lambda (f (\mathfrak{n} ))),\] where this last sum is of lower order in \(\lvert \mathfrak{n} \rvert\). That’s how we use the stable trace formula to induce up the results of Shin–Templier to non-tempered stuff on unitary groups.

GGP conjectures for Bessel periods of unitary groups

Two parts: first by Chaudouard, second by Beuzart-Plessis.

In our works, these conjectures appear as consequences of general theorems on periods of Eisenstein series. These theorems we want to state in the first part of our two talks.

During the talk, we will fix a quadratic extension of number fields \(E/F\). We also fix an integer \(m \geq 1\). We will consider two types of groups. Either a linear group \[G_n = {\mathop{\mathrm{Res}}}_{E/F} \left( {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_n (E) \right).\] Or, if \(\mathcal{H}_n\) denotes the set of isomorphism classes of hermitian forms (nondegenerate) of rank \(n\), then to any such hermitian form \(h \in \mathcal{H}_n\), we attach its group of isomorphisms \[\mathop{\mathrm{U}}(h) := \text{unitary group of } h.\]

To state precisely the theorems about periods of Eisenstein series, first we want to explain which restriction we impose on the representations we are considering. The restrictions are stated in terms of Arthur parameters.

Specifically, we consider regular (hermitian) Arthur parameters (for \(G_n\)), which we call RAP for short. These are, by definition, automorphic representations \(\Pi\) of \(G_n(\mathbb{A})\) that are isobaric sums \[\Pi = \left( \pi_1 \boxplus \pi_1^\ast \right) \boxplus \dotsb \boxplus \left( \pi_r \boxplus \pi_r^\ast \right) \boxplus \Pi_{\mathop{\mathrm{disc}}}.\] Here the \(\pi_i\) are cuspidal representations of some group \(G_{n_i}\), where we assume given some decomposition \[n = n_0 + 2 (n_1 + \dotsb + n_r ).\] Here \(\pi_1^\ast\) is the conjugate dual of \(\pi_i\). We assume also that the representations \(\pi_1 , \pi_1^\ast, \dotsc, \pi_r, \pi_r^\ast\) are pairwise distinct. The discrete part is also an isobaric sum: \[\Pi_{\mathop{\mathrm{disc}}} = \tau_1 \boxplus \dotsb \boxplus \tau_r,\] where \(\tau_i\) is a cuspidal representation of \(G_{m_i }\) for some partition \[n_0 = m_1 + \dotsb + m_{\ell} ,\] with \(\tau_i = \tau_i^\ast\). We can attach to \(\tau_i\) a sign. There is a dichotomy concerning Asai representations: plus and minus. Exactly one of them has a pole at \(s = 1\). We assume that \[L (s, \tau_i, \mathrm{As}^{(- 1 )^{n + 1}}) \text{ has a pole at } s = 1.\] We assume also that \(\tau_1, \dotsc, \tau_{\ell}\) are pairwise distinct.

We say that \(\Pi\) is discrete if it is equal to its discrete part, i.e., \(\Pi = \Pi_{\mathop{\mathrm{disc}}}\).

We can twist our parameters. Set \(a_\Pi := \mathbb{R}^{r}\). Given \(\lambda = (\lambda_1, \dotsc, \lambda_r ) \in a_{\Pi }\), we can consider \[\Pi_\lambda = \pi_1 \lvert \det \rvert^{\lambda_1 } \boxplus \pi_1^\ast \lvert \det \rvert^{- \lambda_1 } \boxplus \dotsb.\]

On the other hand, let \(h \in \mathcal{H}_n\), \(\mathop{\mathrm{U}}:= \mathop{\mathrm{U}}(h)\). Consider pairs \((P, \sigma)\), where \(P\) is a parabolic subgroup of \(U\), and \(\sigma\) is a cuspidal representation of the Levi subgroup \(M_P\). We say that the weak base change \(\mathrm{WBC} (P, \sigma )\) is \(\Pi\) if:

  • We have \[M_P = G_{n_1 } \times \dotsb \times G_{n_r } \times \mathop{\mathrm{U}}(h_0),\] where \[h_0 \in \mathcal{H}_{n_0 }, \qquad h_0 \oplus (\text{hyperbolic spaces}) = h.\]

  • We have \[\sigma = \pi_1 \otimes \dotsb \otimes \pi_r \otimes \sigma_0.\]

  • We say that \(\mathrm{WBC} (\sigma_0) = \Pi_{\mathop{\mathrm{disc}}}\) if for almost all places of \(F\) that split in \(E\), we have \[\sigma_{0, v} \otimes \sigma_{0, v}^\vee \cong \Pi_{\mathop{\mathrm{disc}}, v}.\]

Remark 10. By Ramakrishnan’s result, if \(\Pi\) exists, then it is unique. Moreover, if we add the unitary variant of the work of Arthur due to Mok and KMSW, then “weak base change” implies “base change” in the stronger sense.

We denote by \(\mathfrak{a}_P^\ast\) the space of unramified characters of \(P (\mathbb{A})\). If \(\Pi = \mathrm{WBC} (P, \sigma)\), then \(a_P^\ast \cong a_\Pi^\ast\).

Now we introduce periods. What is the group, and what is the subgroup? We fix, as before, a hermitian space \(h \in \mathcal{H}_n\) of rank \(n\). The bigger group is \[\mathop{\mathrm{U}}:= {\mathop{\mathrm{U}}}_h := \mathop{\mathrm{U}}(h) \times \mathop{\mathrm{U}}(h \oplus E e_0),\] where we add to \(h\) a one-dimensional hermitian space. The subgroup is \[U' := U'_h := \mathop{\mathrm{U}}(h),\] embedded diagonally: \(g \mapsto (g, \mathop{\mathrm{diag}}(g,1))\). We’re going to consider a representation on this product of unitary groups.

We also need regular Arthur parameters, say for \(G_n \times G_{n + 1}\). These are \(\Pi = \Pi_n \otimes \Pi_{n + 1}\), where \(\Pi_i\) are regular Arthur parameters of \(G_i\), and no summand in \(\Pi_n\) is the contragredient of a summand in \(\Pi_{n + 1}\).

Example 11. If \(\Pi_n\) or \(\Pi_{n + 1}\) is discrete, then \(\Pi_n \otimes \Pi_{n + 1}\) is RAP.

Suppose now that \[(P, \sigma ) = (P_n \times P_{n + 1}, \sigma_n \times \sigma_{n + 1})\] is a pair in \(\mathop{\mathrm{U}}\). Then we have \[\mathrm{WBC} (P, \sigma ) = \Pi \iff \mathrm{WBC} (P_i, \sigma_i ) = \Pi_i.\]

Let’s fix \(\Pi\), a regular Arthur parameter for \(G_{n} \times G_{n + 1}\), and \((P, \sigma)\), a pair of \(\mathop{\mathrm{U}}\), such that \(\mathrm{WBC} (P, \sigma ) = \Pi\). We attach the space of automorphic forms \(\mathcal{A}_{P, \sigma }\) inside the space of functions on \(M_P (F) N_P (\mathbb{A} ) \backslash \mathop{\mathrm{U}}(\mathbb{A})\). As a representation, this is the induced representation \({\mathop{\mathrm{Ind}}}_{P (\mathbb{A} ) }^{G (\mathbb{A})} (\sigma)\). To any \(\varphi \in \mathcal{A}_{P, \sigma }\) and \(\lambda \in \mathfrak{a}_P^\ast \otimes \mathbb{C}\), we can form an Eisenstein series \(E (\varphi, \lambda )\), given in some region by a series and in general by meromorphic continuation, which is analytic on the space of unitary unramified characters. We consider the following periods of Eisenstein series \(E (\varphi, \lambda )\): \[\mathcal{P} (\lambda ) : \mathcal{A}_{P, \sigma } \rightarrow \mathbb{C}\] \[\varphi \mapsto \int_{[U']}^\ast E (\varphi, \lambda ), \qquad [U'] = U ' (F) \backslash U' (\mathbb{A}).\] Here \(\int^\ast\) is the regular period defined by Ichino–Yamana. What they did was to introduce a truncation operator \(\Lambda^T_m\) (\(m\) standing for “mixed” and \(T\) a truncation parameter) which has the property that, when applied to Eisenstein series and restricted to \([U']\), one gets rapidly decreasing functions, so one can at least define \[\int_{[U']} \Lambda_m^T E (\varphi, \lambda ).\] What Ichino–Yamana do is study the behavior in \(T\) of this integral. They show that it is a polynomial in \(T\). The purely polynomial part is in fact a constant. This constant should be the regularized integral. But here we are lucky, because we are considering a regular Arthur parameter, especially with the second condition, and with that, this integral in fact does not depend upon \(T\), so remember that we start from a apir \((P, \sigma)\) whose weak base change is \(\Pi\) which is regular, and in this situation, this integral does not depend upon \(T\), and this gives our integral \(\int^\ast\).

For example, if \(\Pi_n\) or \(\Pi_{n + 1}\) is discrete, then the integral makes sense without regularization.

We now have defined enough to state our first theorem, which we call the GGP conjecture for Eisenstein series. (If the parameter \(\Pi\) is discrete, then we recover the usual GGP conjecture.)

Theorem 12 (GGP conjecture for Eisenstein series). Let \(\Pi\) be a regular Arthur parameter, and let \(\lambda \in i \mathfrak{a}_\Pi^\ast\). The following are equivalent:

  1. The Rankin–Selberg \(L\)-function \(L (\tfrac{1}{2}, \Pi_\lambda ) \neq 0\).

  2. We can find \(h \in \mathcal{H}_n\) and \((P, \sigma )\) for \({\mathop{\mathrm{U}}}_h\) such that \[\mathrm{WBC} (P, \sigma) = \Pi\] and \[\mathcal{P} (\lambda) |_{\mathcal{A}_{P, \sigma}} \not \equiv 0.\]

Remark 13. If \(\Pi\) is in fact a cuspidal representation, then this is due to Beuzart-Plessis–Liu–Zhang–Zhu. If \(\Pi\) is discrete, then it is due to Beuzart-Plessis–Chaudouard–Zydor.

We also get some factorizations in the spirit of Ichino–Ikeda. By multiplicity one principle, we have global and local hermitian forms that must agree up to a multiple, and Ichino–Ikeda predicted (at least for orthogonal groups) that the constant of proportionality.

Let \(h \in \mathcal{H}_n\), let \(\Pi\) be a regular Arthur parameter, and let \((P, \sigma)\) with \(\mathrm{WBC} (P, \sigma) = \Pi\). Assume that for all \(v\), \(\sigma_v\) is tempered. We form \[\Sigma_v = {\mathop{\mathrm{Ind}}}_{P (F_v )}^{G (F_v )} (\sigma_v ), \qquad \lambda \in i \mathfrak{a}_P^\ast,\] \[\Sigma_{v, \lambda } = \mathop{\mathrm{Ind}}(\sigma_{\lambda, v}).\] Given \(\varphi_v, \varphi_{v '} \in \Sigma_v\), and an inner product \(\langle , \rangle_v\) on \(\sigma_v\) and on \(\Sigma_v\), we form \[\mathcal{P}_v (\lambda, \varphi_v, \varphi_v ') = \frac{\int_{U' (F_v )} \left( \Sigma_{U, \lambda } (h) \varphi_v, \varphi_v ' \right)_v \, d h }{ \mathcal{L} (\tfrac{1}{2}, \Sigma_{\lambda, v}) }.\] \[\mathcal{L} (s, \Sigma_{\lambda, v}) = \prod_{i = 1 }^{n + 1} L (s + i - 1/2, \eta_v^{\otimes i}) \frac{L (s, \Pi_{\lambda, v})}{ L (s + 1/2, \Pi_{\lambda, v}, \mathrm{As}')},\] where \(\eta = \otimes \eta_v\) is the quadratic character of \(\mathbb{A}_F^\times\) corresponding to \(E/F\), and \[\mathrm{As}' = \mathrm{As}^{(- 1 )^n } \otimes \mathrm{As}^{(- 1)^{n + 1}}.\] We also need to introduce the Petersson inner product. On \(\mathcal{A}_{P, \sigma}\), we define \[\lVert \varphi \rVert^2 = \int_{ A_P^\infty M_P (F) N_P (\mathbb{A} ) \backslash U (\mathbb{A}) } \lvert \varphi (g) \rvert^2 \, d g.\] We use Tamagawa measures, together with a local decomposition \[d g = \otimes_v d g_v.\]

Theorem 14. Let \(\varphi \in \mathcal{A}_{P, \sigma}\) be a factorizable vector \(\varphi = \otimes \varphi_v \in \otimes \Sigma_v\). Then \[\frac{\lvert \mathcal{P} (\varphi, \lambda ) \rvert^2 }{ \lVert \varphi \rVert^2_{\mathrm{Pet}}} = \frac{1}{2^N } \cdot \mathcal{L}^\ast (\tfrac{1}{2}, \Sigma_\lambda ) \cdot \prod_v \frac{\mathcal{P} (\lambda, \varphi_v, \varphi_v )}{ (\varphi_v, \varphi_v )_v }\] where \[\mathcal{L} (s, \Sigma_\lambda ) = \prod_v \mathcal{L} (s, \Sigma_{\lambda, v}), \quad \mathcal{L}^\ast (s, \Sigma_\lambda ) = (s - 1/2 )^{- \dim (\mathfrak{a}_{\Pi} )} \mathcal{L} (s, \Sigma_\lambda).\]

About the proof. It’s a comparison of relative trace formulas. Let \(G\) be a reductive group over \(F\), and \(f\) a test function. Then \[k (x, y) = \sum_{\gamma \in G (F) } f (x^{-1} \gamma y) = \sum_{\chi : \text{cusp data}} k_\chi.\] We have \(G_1, G_2 \circlearrowright G\). We define \[J (f) = \int_{[G_1 ]} \int_{[G_2]} K (x,y) \, d x \, d y,\] after some regularization.

For us, we take

  • \(G = {\mathop{\mathrm{U}}}_h\) and \(G_1 = G_2 = \mathop{\mathrm{U}}'_h\), and

  • \(G = G_{n } \times G_{n + 1}\) and \(G_1 = G_n \hookrightarrow G\) and \(G_2 = \mathop{\mathrm{GL}}(n, F) \times \mathop{\mathrm{GL}}(n + 1, F)\) against something like \(\eta_{G_2}\).

Zydor gives a RTF with coarse expansion \[J (f) = \sum_{\chi } J_\chi (f).\] For the proof, we compute \(J_\chi (f)\). For this, we have the following proposition, due to Zydor in the linear case and to the authors in general. We see that the contribution here attached to the cuspidal datum is related to the truncation that we mentioned.

Proposition 15. \[\int_{[G_1 ] \times [G_2 ]} \left( \Lambda_m^T K_\chi \right) (x, y ) \eta^? \,d x \,d y \sim \text{exponential polynomial},\] where \(\Lambda_m^T\) is the truncation operator of Ichino–Yamana and the right hand side has constant term \(J_\chi (f)\).

Unitary case: \(\chi = (P, \sigma)\), \(\mathrm{WBC} (P, \sigma) = \Pi\) (regular Arthur parameter). \[J_\chi (f) = \int_{i \mathfrak{a}_P^\ast } \sum_{\varphi \in \mathcal{A}_{P, \sigma }} \mathcal{P} \left( \mathop{\mathrm{Ind}}(\sigma_\lambda, f) \varphi, \lambda \right) \overline{\mathcal{P} (\varphi, \lambda )} \, d \lambda.\] In the linear case, we take \[\chi = (Q, \pi ), \quad {\mathop{\mathrm{Ind}}}_Q (\pi) = \Pi : \mathrm{RAP}.\] We could expect that at least in the Langlands decomposition, we have an integral over the bigger space \[J_\chi (f) = \int_{i \mathfrak{a}_{\Pi}^\ast } J (\lambda, f) \, d \lambda.\] Then we have that \(J (\lambda, f)\) is not identically zero if and only if \(L (\tfrac{1}{2}, \Pi_\lambda ) \neq 0\). \[J^{\mathrm{li}} (\lambda, f) = \sum_{h \in \mathcal{H}_n } \sum_{ \substack{ (P, \sigma) \text{ of } {\mathop{\mathrm{U}}}_h \\ \mathrm{WBC} (P, \sigma) = \Pi } } J^{{\mathop{\mathrm{U}}}_h } (\lambda, f^h ),\] where \({\mathop{\mathrm{Ind}}}_Q (\pi) = \Pi\): RAP and \[f \leftrightarrow (f_h )_{h \in \mathcal{H}_n }\] have local nonvanishing orbital integrals.

On to part two!

Let \(E/F\) be a quadratic extension of number fields. Let \(V_m \subset V_{n + 1}\) be hermitian spaces of dimensions \(m\) and \(n + 1\), respectively. We assume that \(V_m^\perp\) is odd-dimensional and quasi-split. Explicitly, this means that we can decompose this orthogonal complement as a direct sum, as follows: \[V_m^\perp = \left( Y \oplus Y^\ast \right) \oplus D,\] where \(D\) is a hermitian line and \(Y, Y^*\) are maximal isotropic subspaces which are in duality by the hermitian form \(h (\cdot, \cdot )\). We denote by \[U_m := \mathop{\mathrm{U}}(V_m), \qquad {\mathop{\mathrm{U}}}_{n + 1} = \mathop{\mathrm{U}}(V_{n + 1}).\] To this data, we can associate a Bessel subgroup and then a Bessel period.

We need to introduce more groups.

Let \[P = {\mathop{\mathrm{Stab}}}_{U_{n+1} } \left( \langle y_1 \rangle \subsetneq \langle y_1, y_2 \rangle \subsetneq \dotsb \subsetneq \langle y_{1}, \dotsc, y_r \rangle \right)\] be the parabolic subgroup attached to a complete flag in \(Y\). In matrix form, \[P = M N = \begin{pmatrix} \ast & \ast & \ast \\ 0 & \mathop{\mathrm{U}}(D \oplus V_m ) & \ast \\ 0 & 0 & \ast \\ \end{pmatrix}.\] We also define \[\psi_N : [N] = N(F) \backslash N (\mathbb{A} ) \rightarrow \mathbb{C}^\times ,\] \[\psi_N (u) = \psi \left( \sum_{i = 1}^r h (u y_{i + 1}, y_i^\ast) \right),\] where \(\langle y_{r + 1} \rangle = D\) and \(y_i^\ast\) denotes the dual basis of \(Y^\ast\) and \(\psi : \mathbb{A}_E / E \rightarrow \mathbb{C}^\times\). Bessel subgroup: \[\mathcal{B} = {\mathop{\mathrm{U}}}_m \ltimes N \xrightarrow{\mathrm{diag}} \mathcal{G} = {\mathop{\mathrm{U}}}_m \times {\mathop{\mathrm{U}}}_{n + 1},\] \[\psi_{\mathcal{B} } = 1 \otimes \psi_N.\] The Bessel period is then \[P_{\mathcal{B} } : \mathcal{A}_{\mathop{\mathrm{cusp}}} (\mathcal{G} ) \rightarrow \mathbb{C}\] \[\phi \mapsto \int_{[\mathcal{B} ]} \phi (h) \psi_{\mathcal{B} } (h) \, d h.\]

We turn to Rankin–Selberg \(L\)-functions. We consider \[\pi = \pi_m \boxtimes \pi_{n + 1} \hookrightarrow \mathcal{A}_{\mathop{\mathrm{cusp}}} (\mathcal{G} ).\] We denote by \[\mathrm{bc}(\pi ) := \mathrm{bc} (\pi_m ) \boxtimes \mathrm{bc} (\pi_{n + 1}) \hookrightarrow \mathcal{A} ({\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_{m, E} \times {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_{n + 1, E})\] the quadratic base change of \(\pi\). (A priori, it is not cuspidal.) The base change is known to exist in full generality by work of Mok and Kaletha–Minguez–Shin–White. We then set \[L (s, \mathrm{bc} (\pi) ) := L (s, \mathrm{bc} (\pi_m ) \times \mathrm{bc} (\pi_{n + 1})).\]

We turn to pure inner forms. Given another \(m\)-dimensional hermitian space \(V_m '\), one can first construct a bigger hermitian space \(V_{m + 1}'\) by requiring that \[V '_{n + 1} \supset V_m ', \qquad (V_m ')^\perp \cong V_m^\perp.\] This gives rise to \[\mathcal{B}^{V_m '} \hookrightarrow \mathcal{G}^{V_m '}, \quad P_{\mathcal{B}^{V_m '}}.\]

Theorem 16 (GGP conjecture). Assume that \(\mathrm{bc}(\pi)\) is generic. Then the following are equivalent:

  1. \(L (\tfrac{1}{2}, \mathrm{bc} (\pi)) \neq 0\).

  2. There exists \(V_m '\) (with \(\dim (V_m ') = m\)) such that \[\pi ' \hookrightarrow \mathcal{A}_{\mathop{\mathrm{cusp}}} (\mathcal{G}^{V_m '}),\] \[\mathrm{bc} (\pi ') = \mathrm{bc} (\pi),\] \[\mathcal{P}_{\mathcal{B}^{V_m '}} |_{\pi '} \not \equiv 0.\]

Remark 17. Jiang–Zhang have shown that \((ii)\) implies \((i)\), using a different method (a generalization of the descent method). On the contrary, our proof uses relative trace formula techniques.

Theorem 19 (Ichino–Ikeda conjecture). Assume that \(\pi\) is tempered everywhere (i.e., tempered at all places). Then for \[\varphi = \otimes_v ' \varphi_v \in \pi = \otimes_v ' \pi_v,\] we have \[\frac{\left\lvert P_{\mathcal{B} } (\varphi) \right\rvert^2 }{ \langle \varphi, \varphi \rangle_{\mathrm{Pet}} } = 2^{- \beta } \Delta^S \frac{L^S (\tfrac{1}{2}, \mathrm{bc} (\pi) )}{L^S (1, \pi, \mathop{\mathrm{Ad}})} \prod_{v \in S} \frac{ P_{\mathcal{B}, v} (\varphi_v, \varphi_v ) }{\langle \varphi_v, \varphi_v \rangle_v },\] where

  • \(\Delta^S = \prod_{i = 1 }^{n + 1} L^S (i, \eta^i_{E/F})\),

  • \(\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle\) is an invariant inner product on \(\pi\),

  • \(P_{\mathcal{B}, v} (\varphi_v, \varphi_v ) = \int_{\mathcal{B}_v }^{\mathop{\mathrm{reg}}} \langle \pi_v (h) \varphi_v, \varphi_v \rangle_v \psi_{\mathcal{B}, v} (h) \, d h\).

  • We have \[\frac{P_{\mathcal{B}, v} (\varphi_v, \varphi_v)}{ \langle \varphi_v, \varphi_v \rangle_v } = \Delta_v \frac{L (\tfrac{1}{2}, \mathrm{bc} (\pi_v ))}{ L (1, \pi_v , \mathop{\mathrm{Ad}})}\] for almost all \(v\).

  • \(2^\beta = \left\lvert S_\pi \right\rvert\), where \(S_\pi\) is supposed to be the centralizer of the hypothetical Langlands parameter of \(\pi\), but may be defined more concretely by writing \[\mathrm{bc} (\pi_m ) = \sigma_1 \boxplus \dotsb \boxplus \sigma_a,\] \[\mathrm{bc} (\pi_{n + 1}) = \rho_1 \boxplus \dotsb \boxplus \rho_b,\] where the \(\sigma_i\) are cuspidal conjugate self-dual; in that case, \(S_\pi \cong (\mathbb{Z} / 2)^{a + b}\).

We now start the second part of the talk, where we explain how we prove these theorems. There is actually a reduction to corank one periods. We need to introduce one further hermitian space. Recall that we have \(V_m\) and \(V_{n + 1}\). We will include between the two the space \[V_n = V_m \oplus (Y \oplus Y^\ast ) \subset V_{n + 1} = V_n \oplus D,\] where the outer sums are orthogonal. It’s a maximal unipotent subgroup. We also take \[\psi_{\mathcal{B}^L } = \psi_{N_Y} \otimes \psi_{\mathcal{B}},\] where \(\psi_{N_Y}\) is generic. We obtain the Whittaker–Bessel period \[P_{\mathcal{B}^L } : \mathcal{A} (L) \rightarrow \mathbb{C}\] \[\phi \mapsto \int_{[\mathcal{B}^L ] } \phi (h) \psi_{\mathcal{B}^L } (h) \, d h.\] Let us now recall a bit where we started, and then tell you where we’re going. We started with a cuspidal automorphic representation \[\pi = \pi_m \boxtimes \pi_{m + 1} \hookrightarrow \mathcal{A}_{\mathop{\mathrm{cusp}}} (\mathcal{G}).\] We now want to extend it to the Levi \(M\). We fix an automorphic representation \[\tau \hookrightarrow \mathcal{A} ({\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_E (Y)).\] We also need to consider the twists \[\tau_s := \tau \lvert \det \rvert^s,\] taking \(s\) large enough to make the Eisenstein series convergent. Density then implies optimal lifting.

\[\pi_q : {\mathop{\mathrm{SL}}}_n (\mathbb{Z} ) \rightarrow {\mathop{\mathrm{SL}}}_n (\mathbb{Z} / q \mathbb{Z}).\] By strong approximation, this is onto.

Given \(x \in {\mathop{\mathrm{SL}}}_n (\mathbb{Z} / q \mathbb{Z})\), want \(\gamma \in {\mathop{\mathrm{SL}}}_n (\mathbb{Z})\) such that \(\pi_q (\gamma ) = x\), with \(\lVert \gamma \rVert\) minimal. We have \[\lvert {\mathop{\mathrm{SL}}}_n (\mathbb{Z} / q \mathbb{Z} ) \rvert = q^{n - 1 + o (1)}.\] It follows from the work of Duke–Rudnick–Sarnak that \[\# \left\{ \gamma \in {\mathop{\mathrm{SL}}}_n (\mathbb{Z} ) : \lVert \gamma \rVert \leq T \right\} \sim \mathop{\mathrm{vol}}\left\{ g \in {\mathop{\mathrm{SL}}}_n (\mathbb{R} ) : \lVert g \rVert \leq T \right\} \sim C_n T^{n^2 - n}.\]

To get a good asymptotic, expect to need to take \[T^{n^2 - n} \gg q^{n^2 - 1},\] which translates to \[T \geq q^{1 + 1 / n + o(1)}.\]

Theorem 29 (Jana–Kamber, following Assing–Blomer). For each \(\varepsilon> 0\), we have for \(q\) large enough that for all \(x \in {\mathop{\mathrm{SL}}}_n (\mathbb{Z} / q \mathbb{Z} )\), except for at most \(\varepsilon\left\lvert {\mathop{\mathrm{SL}}}_n (\mathbb{Z} / q \mathbb{Z} ) \right\rvert\) many \(\gamma \in {\mathop{\mathrm{SL}}}_n (\mathbb{Z})\), there exists \(\gamma\) with \(\pi_q (\gamma ) = x\) such that \(\lVert \gamma \rVert \leq q^{1 + 1/n + \varepsilon}\).

Let’s now discuss optimal lifting. Let \[\Gamma \leq G \hookrightarrow {\mathop{\mathrm{SL}}}_n (\mathbb{R})\] be an arithmetic group (semisimple, irreducible). Let \(\Gamma_q \leq \Gamma\) be congruence lattices. Then we can more-or-less ask the same questions, but this time we look at the action of \(\Gamma\) on the set \(\Gamma / \Gamma_q\). We get an action \[\Gamma \circlearrowright \Gamma / \Gamma_q,\] and we have \[\# \left\{ \gamma \in \Gamma : \lVert \gamma \rVert \leq T \right\} \asymp \mathop{\mathrm{vol}}\left\{ g \in G : \lVert g \rVert \leq T \right\} \asymp T^\alpha \log^B T.\]

Conjecture 2. For each \(\varepsilon> 0\), for \(q\) large enough, assuming that \(T^\alpha \geq [\Gamma : \Gamma_q ]^{1 + \varepsilon}\), for all \((x, y ) \in \Gamma / \Gamma_q\) except for at most \(\varepsilon\lvert \Gamma / \Gamma_q \rvert^2\) many, there exists \(\gamma \in \Gamma\) such that \(\gamma \cdot x = y\) and \(\lVert \gamma \rVert \leq T\).

Note that if \(\Gamma_q\) is normal, then we get a statement concerning “for all \(x\), for almost all \(y\).”

In particular, for \(T^\alpha \geq [\Gamma : \Gamma_q ]\), we can lift all elements.
