Chris Skinner

In this workshop, we have the various aspects of automorphic forms:

  • analytic,

  • arithmetic,

  • geometric.

In this talk, we want to discuss the role of (automorphic) periods. Let \(G\) be a reductive group over a number field \(F\). Let \(H \leq G\) be a subgroup. Period means that we have an automorphic form \(\varphi\) on \(G(\mathbb{A}_F)\) and that we consider \[\int_{[H]} \varphi(h) \, d h := \int_{H(F) \backslash H(\mathbb{A}_F)} \varphi(h) \, d h,\] or more generally \(\int_{[H]} \varphi(h) \psi(h) \, d h\) for some smooth function \(\psi\).

We want to explain today, focusing on some examples, how the geometry mediates between the analysis and the arithmetic. This often comes from interpreting these periods geometrically. The arithmetic can in turn feed back through the geometry to get some handle on analytic facts that we could not otherwise.

Let \(T \subset {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_2 / \mathbb{Q}\) be a torus. Then, either

  1. \(T\) is split over \(\mathbb{Q}\), hence isomorphic to \(\mathbb{G}_m^2\), or

  2. \(T\) is non-split over \(\mathbb{Q}\), hence identifiable with \(K^\times\) for an embedding of a quadratic extension \(K /\mathbb{Q}\).

In case \((i)\), let \(\pi\) be a cuspidal automorphic representation of \({\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_2\), and let \(\varphi \in \pi\). Then \[\int_{[T]} \varphi(t) \chi(t) \lvert t_1 / t_2 \rvert^s \, d^\times t \, \dot{=}\, L(\pi \otimes \chi, s + \tfrac{1}{2}).\] Classically, this looks like \[\int_0^\infty f(i y ) y^s \, \frac{d y}{y} ={(2 \pi)}^{- s} \Gamma(s) L(f, s).\] More generally, for a Dirichlet character \(\chi\) modulo \(M\), \[\label{eq:cnej2gzxf2}\tag{1} \sum_{a (M)} \bar{\chi}(a) \int_0^\infty f(i y + \tfrac{a}{M} ) y^s \, \frac{d y}{y} =(2 \pi )^{- s} \tau(\bar{\chi})\Gamma(s) L(f \otimes \chi, s).\] If \(f\) is of weight \(2\), then \[f(\tau) \, d \tau =: \omega_f\] is a differential on the modular curve \(X := X_1(N)\). We can think of the geodesics \([0,i \infty]\) and more generally \([ \tfrac{a}{M}, i \infty ]\) as defining elements of \(H_1(X, \mathbb{Z})\) (“modular symbols”). Indeed, they span this. We can write the left hand side of \((1)\) in the form \[\left\langle \Sigma \left[ \frac{a}{M} \right] \bar{\chi}(a), \omega_f \right\rangle.\] For a newform \(f= \sum a_n q^n\), we can ask either that \(a_n \in \mathbb{Q}\) or \(a_n \in \mathbb{Z}\).

One can define periods \[\Omega_f^{\pm} \in \mathbb{C}^\times\] such that \[\eta_f^{\pm} := \frac{1}{ \Omega_f^{\pm} } \left( \omega_f \pm \overline{\omega_f} \right)\] is integral.

We obtain results of the form \[\tau(\overline{\chi}) \frac{L(f, \chi, 1)}{(2 \pi i) \Omega_f^{\pm}} \in \mathbb{Z} [\chi],\] where \(\chi(-1) = \pm 1\). This gives nonvanishing of \(L(f, \chi, 1)\) for many \(\chi\).

Question 1. Which kinds of characters \(\chi\) do you need?

Rohrlich. Fix a prime \(p\). There exists a \(\chi\) of conductor \(p^t\), for some \(t\), such that \(L(f, \chi, 1) \neq 0\).

For \(p\) odd, we have \((\mathbb{Z} / p^t)^\times \cong (\mathbb{Z} / p)^\times \times \mathbb{Z} / p^{t - 1}\). We can ask that the “tame” part of \(\chi\) (i.e., the restriction to \({(\mathbb{Z} / p)}^\times\)) be fixed and sitll get such a nonvanishing result.

Manin–Vishuk/Mazur–Swinnerton-Dyer/Mazur–Tate–Tefebbaum: uses modular symbols to construct \(p\)-adic \(L\)-functions: \[L_p(f, \chi) ={(\ast)}_{\chi, f, p} \frac{L(f, \chi, 1)}{ \Omega_f^{\pm}}\] where \(\chi\) is of conductor \(p^t\) and the factor \((\ast)\) is very explicit. Writing \[\varprojlim {(\mathbb{Z} / p^t)}^\times \cong {(\mathbb{Z} / p )}^\times \times \mathbb{Z}_p,\] we have that \(L_p(f, \chi )\), viewed through the \(\chi\) argument as a function of the second factor \(\mathbb{Z}_p\) in the above isomorphism, is analytic, hence is essentially a polynomial. This allows us to get stronger nonvanishing results, e.g., concerning all but finitely many \(\chi\).

There have been some efforts to generalize the theory of modular symbols, e.g., to a reductive group \(G\) and the period functional defined by certain subgroups \(H\). For example:

  • \({\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_2\) over a number field

  • \({\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_n \times {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_{n - 1}\), Rankin–Selberg \(\pi \otimes \sigma\) (Kazhdan–Mazur–Schmidt).

  • \({\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_{2 n} \supset H = \left( \begin{smallmatrix} {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_n&\\ &{\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_n \\ \end{smallmatrix} \right)\), Shalika model.

Another way to get a handle on these sorts of results is through Rankin–Selberg integrals, e.g., \[\frac{\left\langle f, E_{\chi, \psi }, E_{{(\chi \psi)}^{-1}, 1}\right\rangle}{\left\langle f, f \right\rangle} \, \dot{=} \, \frac{L(f, \chi, 1) L(f, \psi, 1)}{\Omega_f^+ \Omega_f^- }.\] Then one can play the game: given \(\chi\), choose a suitable \(\psi\).

Let’s turn now to direction \((ii)\), where we have \(K^\times \hookrightarrow {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_2\). Let’s even focus on the case where \(K/\mathbb{Q}\) is imaginary quadratic. More generally, we can replace \({\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_2\) with \(B^\times\) for a quaternion algebra \(B\). There are always these issues of when you have an embedding into a paritcular quaternion algebra, which is interesting arithmetic of itself. The geometry then involves an adelic quotient \[X := B^\times \backslash {(B \otimes \mathbb{A})}^\times / \mathbb{R}_{> 0} K _\infty^0 U, \quad U \leq {(B \otimes \mathbb{A}_f)}^\times.\] There are two cases:

  1. If it’s split at the archimedean place, then we get a curve.

  2. If it’s isomorphic to Hamilton’s quaternions at the archimedean place, then we get a finite set.

We get an induced map \[\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{Q}}^\times \backslash \mathbb{A}_K^\times \rightarrow X.\] The image is finite, consisting of certain CM or “special” points, defined over ray class fields of \(K\).

The period integrals \[\label{eq:cnej2m5ln6}\tag{2} \left\lvert \int_{[K^\times]} \varphi(t) \chi(t) \, d^\times t \right\rvert^2 \, \dot{=} \, L(\mathrm{BC}_{\mathbb{Q}}^K(\pi) \otimes \chi, \tfrac{1}{2}).\] (Of course we need the compatibility condition \(\chi|_{\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}} \chi_\pi = 1\) for the integral to make sense.)

In case \((b)\), we have the map \(\mathbb{A}_K^\times \rightarrow X\), and as we have results to the effect that the image becomes equidistributed as \(\dim K \rightarrow \infty\) (Templier).

Results of Vatsal, where the geometry and ergodic theory give results that one doesn’t know how to obtain via traditional analytic methods.

Example \((ii)\) fits into the Gan–Gross–Prasad framework.

  • \(\mathop{\mathrm{U}}(2) \times \mathop{\mathrm{U}}(1)\) relates via periods to \(L(\pi \otimes \chi, \tfrac{1}{2})\).

  • \(\mathop{\mathrm{U}}(n) \times \mathop{\mathrm{U}}(n - 1)\) relates via periods to \(L(\pi_1 \otimes \pi_2, \tfrac{1}{2})\).

We can vary these in families. We can mix the two, by taking the automorphic representations to be more degenerate, possibly coming from endoscopy of automorphic representations of smaller groups, e.g., characters.

The \(\mathop{\mathrm{U}}(n) \times \mathop{\mathrm{U}}(n-1)\) \(L\)-functions show up as auxiliary factors in the construction of Euler systems.

Hida: apply \((2)\) but for an Eisenstein series, use that to study \(L\)-values for \(\chi\) in certain families. Scope for generalizing this.

Philippe Michel

Now we say something about how analysts try to understand questions concerning, e.g., nonvanishing of \(L\)-functions.

\[L(\pi, s ) = \sum_{n \geq 1} \frac{\lambda_\pi(n)}{ n^s } = \prod_p L_p(\pi, s ), \quad L_p(\pi, s ) = \prod_{i = 1 }^d \left( 1 - \frac{\alpha_{\pi, i}(p)}{ p^s } \right)^{-1}.\]

There is also the archimedean factor \[L _\infty(\pi, s) = \prod_{i = 1 }^d \Gamma_{\mathbb{R} } (s - \mu_{\pi, i })\] and the functional equation \[\Lambda(s) := L _\infty L(s) = \varepsilon(\pi) q^{1/2 - s} \Lambda(\bar{\pi}, 1 - s)\] and the analytic conductor \[q_\pi \prod_{i = 1 }^d \left( 1 + \lvert \mu_{\pi, i } - s \rvert \right).\] We have \[L(\pi, s ) \, \dot{=} \, P(\varphi, \chi, s) = \int_{[H]} \varphi(h) \chi_s(h) \, d h.\]

Size of \(L(\pi, s)\)? Focus on \(\Re s = \tfrac{1}{2}\).

The convexity bound asserts that \[L(\pi, s) \ll C(\pi, s )^{1/4 + o (1)}.\] The subconvexity problem is to improve \(1/4\) to \(1 /4 - \delta\) for some \(\delta > 0\). The first case is due to Hermann Weyl in 1921 for \(L(s) = \zeta(s)\), who showed that one can replace \(1/4\) with \(1/6\).

How about the nonvanishing problem? Suppose given a family \[\mathcal{F}_Q = \left\{ \pi : C(\pi, s) \asymp Q \right\}.\] As \(Q \rightarrow \infty\), find \(\pi\) such that \(L(\pi, \tfrac{1}{2}) \neq 0\)? Then, determine how many of them there are, and also consider subfamilies.

Example 2. \({\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_1\) twists. \(\mathcal{F}_Q = \left\{ \pi_0 \times \chi \mid\vert \chi \text{ on } F^\times \backslash \mathbb{A}_F^\times, \, C(\chi ) \asymp Q \right\}\). \[M_1(Q) := \sum_{\pi \in \mathcal{F}_Q } L(\pi, \tfrac{1}{2} ) \neq 0 \text{ for } Q \rightarrow \infty.\]

Note that subconvexity improves upon nonvanishing: \[\left\lvert \left\{ \pi : L(\pi, \tfrac{1}{2} ) \neq 0 \right\} \right\rvert \gg \frac{\lvert \mathcal{F}_Q \rvert^{1 + o(1)}}{Q^{1/4 - \delta }}.\]

Another way to get at nonvanishing is to consider also a second moment: \[M_2(Q) := \sum_{\pi \in \mathcal{F}_Q } \lvert L(\pi, \tfrac{1}{2} ) \rvert^2 = \lvert \mathcal{F}_Q \rvert^{1 + o(1)}?\] If one can show this asymptotic, then one deduces \[\left\lvert \left\{ \pi : L(\pi, \tfrac{1}{2} ) \neq 0 \right\} \right\rvert = \lvert \mathcal{F}_Q \rvert^{1 - o(1)}.\]

A basic tool for doing this sort of analysis is the approximate functional equation: \[L(\pi, \tfrac{1}{2} ) = \sum_n \frac{\lambda_\pi(n) }{ n^{1/2} } V \left( \frac{n}{X} \right) + \varepsilon(\chi) \sum_n \frac{\overline{\lambda_\pi(n) }}{ n^{1/2} } W \left( \frac{n}{Y} \right).\] Here \(X Y = Q\). One could take, for instance, \(X = Y = \sqrt{Q}\); then each sum would have length roughly \(\sqrt{Q}\). One faces in this way \[\sum_{\pi \in \mathcal{F}_Q} \lambda_\pi(n), \quad \sum_{\pi \in \mathcal{F}_Q } \lambda_\pi(m) \overline{\lambda_\pi(n)} \varepsilon(\pi)\] which gives rise to \[\sum_{\chi(q)} \chi(m) \overline{\chi(n)} = \varphi(q) \delta_{m \equiv n(q)}\] and more generally one gets stuff that one can study using trace formulas.

Another approach is to write \(L(\pi, \tfrac{1}{2})\) as an automorphic period and then to use the RTF.

Variant: twisted moments \[\sum_\pi L(\pi, \tfrac{1}{2} ) \lambda_\pi(\ell ).\] This can be useful to single out some \(\pi\) inside \(\mathcal{F}_Q\). It can sometimes be used to prove nonvanishing for a positive proportion of \(\pi\), i.e., \[\left\lvert \left\{ \pi \in \mathcal{F}_Q : L(\pi, \tfrac{1}{2} ) \neq 0 \right\} \right\rvert \geq c \lvert \mathcal{F}_Q \rvert, \quad c > 0.\] Mollification method: \[\sum L(\pi, \tfrac{1}{2}) m(\pi, L), \quad m(\pi, L) := \sum_{\ell \leq L} \frac{\mu(\ell) \lambda_\pi(\ell)}{\ell^{1/2}}.\] Here \(m(\pi,L)\) is called a mollifier. One takes \(L := Q^\alpha\) with \(\alpha > 0\) fixed. It’s chosen so that we’ll have \[L(\pi, \tfrac{1}{2} ) m(\pi, L) = 1 + \sum_{m \geq L} \frac{\lambda_\pi(m)}{m^{1/2}}.\] This gap in the coefficients is what allows you to get a positive proportion. (Selberg’s work on the positive proportion of zeros of zeta on the critical line. Iwaniec–Sarnak’s paper on nonvanishing.)

Chinta: for \(f\) of weight \(2\), and \(q\) prime, \[\left\lvert \left\{ \chi(q) : L(f \times \chi, \tfrac{1}{2} ) \neq 0 \right\} \right\rvert = q - 1 + \operatorname{O}\left( q^{7/8 + \varepsilon} \right).\] This is a very strong proportion of nonvanishing. Nothing like this is available for, e.g., Maass forms.

Let’s now talk about the subconvexity problem. This is ostensibly about a single \(L\)-function, but many proofs of estimates use families. Suppose we want to show \[L(\pi_0, \tfrac{1}{2} ) \ll C(\pi_0 )^{1/4 - \delta}, \quad \delta > 0.\] Suppose that \(L(\pi_0, \tfrac{1}{2} ) \geq 0\). For \(\pi_0 \in \mathcal{F}_Q\), one expects \[L(\pi_0, \tfrac{1}{2} ) \leq \sum_{\pi \in \mathcal{F}_Q} L(\pi, \tfrac{1}{2} ) \ll \lvert \mathcal{F}_Q \rvert^{1 + o (1)}.\] If \(\lvert \mathcal{F}_Q \rvert \leq Q^{1/4 - \delta}\), then one solves the problem. A spectacular example can be found in the work of Conrey–Iwaniec and Petrow–Young. They show that for a Dirichlet character \(\chi\) of conductor \(q\), \[L(\chi, \tfrac{1}{2} ) \ll q^{1/6 + o(1)}.\] In this case, we don’t have nonnegativity, so they pass to \[{\left( L(\chi, \tfrac{1}{2} ) L(\bar{\chi}, \tfrac{1}{2} ) \right)}^3 = {L(\chi \boxplus \bar{\chi}, \tfrac{1}{2} )}^3 = L\left(3 \boxtimes \left( \chi \boxplus \bar{\chi} \right), \tfrac{1}{2} \right).\] They work with the family \[\left\{ 3 \boxtimes(f \times \chi ) : f \in S(q, \bar{\chi}^2) \right\}.\]

It often happens that \[\lvert \mathcal{F}_Q \rvert^{1 + o(1)} = Q^{1/4}.\] In such cases, amplification sometimes works. \[m_{\pi_0}(L) L(\pi_0, \tfrac{1}{2}) \leq \sum L(\pi, \tfrac{1}{2}) m_\pi(L)^2 \ll \lvert \mathcal{F}_Q \rvert^{1 + o(1)} = Q^{1/4},\] \[m_\pi(L) := \sum_{\ell \leq L} \frac{x_{\ell} \lambda_\pi(\ell)}{\ell^{1/2}}, \quad m_{\pi_0 } \gg L^\alpha , \quad 0 < \alpha \leq 1/2.\] One takes \(L\) to be a small enough power of \(Q\).

Now let’s talk about an application, returning to the setting of Chris’s talk. Take \(B/\mathbb{Q}\) a quaternion algebra. Take \(G = {\mathop{\mathrm{PB}}}^\times \supset T \cong {\mathop{\mathrm{Res}}}_{K / \mathbb{Q} } \mathbb{G}_m / \mathbb{G}_m\), and \(\chi\) on \(T\) of weight zero. Take \(\pi_{\mathrm{JL}}\) on \(G\). Then \[\frac{L(\pi_K \times \chi, \tfrac{1}{2} )}{ C(\pi_K \times \chi)^{1/4}} \asymp \left\lvert \int_{[T]} \varphi(t) \chi(t) \, d t \right\rvert^2.\] How to get something like surjectivity of quotiented-versions of the map \([T] \rightarrow [G]\), as mentioned in Chris’s talk? Average over \(\chi\): \[\sum_\chi \frac{L(\pi_K \times \chi, \tfrac{1}{2} )}{ C(\pi_K \times \chi)^{1/4}} \asymp \sum_\chi \left\lvert \int_{[T]} \varphi(t) \chi(t) \, d t \right\rvert^2 = h_c \int_{[T]} \lvert \varphi \rvert^2\] by Parseval. If one can show that \(\int_{[T]} \lvert \varphi \rvert^2 > 0\) as \(T \rightarrow \infty\), then it will prove that at least one of the \(L\)-values on the left hand side does not vanish. One has the equidistribution theorem of Duke, to the effect that \([T] \subset [G]\) is essentially equidistributed. This means typically that \(\int_{[T]} \lvert \varphi \rvert^2\) converges to \(\int_{[G]} \lvert \varphi \rvert^2\), which gives the required nonvanishing. One can understand this spectrally: \[\int_{[T]} \lvert \varphi \rvert^2 = \int_{[T]} \sum_\psi \langle \lvert \varphi \rvert^2, \psi \rangle \psi\] then use that \[\int_{[T]} \psi(t) \, d t \xrightarrow{?} \int_{[G]} \psi.\]

Vatsal setting: \[\int_T \varphi(t) \bar{\varphi}(t_1 t) \, d t,\] where \(t_1\) is growing a lot with \(p^k\), \(k \geq 0\). Here \(t_1^h = 1\), where \(h = h_K\) is the class number. Vatsal used homogeneous dynamics, Ratner theory.

We can now obtain similar assertions even when the variation is not vertical, but even when it is more oblique or horizontal. This uses new analytic techniques and homogeneous dynamics. Techniques of Holowinsky–Soundararajan, Einsiedler–Lindenstrauss. One thing this uses is that \(\lvert \lambda_\pi(n) \rvert\) is a multiplicative function.

  13. In the setting of question \((5)\), when is the space of \(K_H(p^n)\)-fixed vectors one-dimensional? Is there always an \(H\) for which this happens? Is there a commonality between such \(H\)?

Let’s talk about vanishing of \(L(\pi, \tfrac{1}{2})\) and \(L(\pi \chi, \tfrac{1}{2})\).

There is a dichotomy according as \(C(\chi) = p^m\) (Iwasawa) or \(C(\chi) = \ell\) (a prime tending off to \(\infty\)).

Let \(f\) be a modular form of weight two that is ordinary at \(p\). Let \(\chi\) have conductor \(p^m\). Then \[L_p(f, \chi) = \frac{L(f, \chi, 1)}{\Omega_f^{\pm}} \times(\text{extra factors}).\] If there exists \(\chi\) such that \(L_p(f, \chi) \neq 0\), then only finitely many \(\chi\) can have \(L(f, \chi, 1) = 0\). This uses the fact that \(L_p(f, \chi)\) is a power series on \(\mathbb{Z}_p^\times\).

The formula is that \[L_p(f, \chi) = \mathrm{const} \times \int_{ \substack{ \mathrm{aut. cycle} \\ \text{of level } n \geq C(\chi) } } \phi_\alpha \left( \begin{pmatrix} x p^n & x \\ 0 & 1 \\ \end{pmatrix} \right) \chi(x) \,d^\times x.\] Here \(\phi_\alpha \in \pi\), where \(\alpha\) is an ordinary \(U_p\)-eigenvector.

What kinds of objects are \(p\)-adic \(L\)-functions in general? Sprawling conjectures of Coates, developed by Perrin–Riou. \(p\)-adic \(L\)-functions are conjecturally attached to certain kinds of polarized Galois representations. The fact that they are geometric is a consequence of the fact that they are attached to automorphic forms. The polarization is a choice of one of the two \(U_p\)-eigenvalues in the previous setting.

Let’s turn to the automorphic language. Let \(\pi\) be a cuspidal representation of \({\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_n / \mathbb{Q}\). Let \(\lambda\) be an algebraic weight of \(\pi\). Typically, that amounts to the local representation \(\pi_p\) being spherical (Coates) or \(\pi_p^{\mathrm{Iw}} \neq 0\). We’ll restrict ourselves in this setting, although there are other examples (see Hida).

In terms of local representation theory, the fact that you have vectors fixed by the Iwahori group says that \(\pi_p\) is included in \({\mathop{\mathrm{Ind}}}_B^{{\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_n} \theta\) of an unramified character \(\theta\). A refinement of \(\pi\) is a choice \(\tilde{\pi}\) of \(\theta\).

Conjecturally, there should exist \(\mathcal{L}_p(\tilde{\pi}) \in \operatorname{Dist}(\mathbb{Z}_p^\times, \mathbb{C}_p)\) such that if \(\chi\) has conductor \(p^n\) and \(s \in \mathbb{Z}_p\), then \[L_p(\tilde{\pi}, \chi, s) = \int_{\mathbb{Z}_p^\times} \left\langle x \right\rangle^{s - 1} \chi(x) \, d \mathcal{L}_p(\tilde{\pi})\] such that whenever \(j\) is a Deligne critical integer, we have \[\label{eq:cnfg47dn34}\tag{3} L_p(\tilde{\pi}, \chi, j) = \frac{L(\pi \times \chi, j + \tfrac{1}{2})}{\mathrm{period}} \times(\text{extra factors})_{\tilde{\pi}, \chi, j}.\]

Remark 21. Need to control power of \(p\)-adic logarithm \(\log_p\) in \(L_p\).

Example 22 (Bellaïche). Let \(f\) be a CM modular form of weight \(k\). Let \(f_\alpha\) be a refinement in the image of Coleman’s \(\theta^{k - 1}\). Then for any critical integer \(j\), i.e., \(1 \leq j \leq k - 1\), we have \[L_p(f_\alpha, \chi, j) = 0 \quad \text{ for all } \chi.\] The reason this occurs is because \(L_p\) is divisible by \(\prod \log_p\).

For this reason, we should also impose the fact that \[L_p = \operatorname{O}\left( \log_p^h \right)\] for some \(h\). That is to say, we need some control over the growth. If we can do that in this setup, with \[h = \nu_p \left( U_p \text{-eigenvalue} \right),\] Then \((3)\), for all \(\chi\), uniquely determines \(L_p\) as long as \(h \leq \# (\text{critical integers})\) (e.g., \(k - 1\) for \(S_k\)).

In fact, don’t need all \(\chi\); suffices to know this for all sufficiently ramified \(\chi\) (Nguyen).

You construct \[\mathcal{L}_p(\tilde{\pi}) = \text{constant term of an Eisenstein series}.\] This is technical but very powerful.

  3. Geometric approach of Stevens. This is what we’ll discuss a bit more.

\(\mathcal{L}_p(\tilde{\pi})\) is constructed as follows: \[K_{\tilde{\pi}} \in H_c^\ast(X_K, (\text{some locally analytic distributions})_\lambda).\] Take \[\mathcal{L}_p(\tilde{\pi}) = \text{image of } \int_{\text{aut. cycle}} K \tilde{\pi} \in \operatorname{Dist}(M_n(\mathbb{Z}_p) \rightarrow \operatorname{Dist}(\mathbb{Z}_p^\times)).\] You can show that this is equal to \(\operatorname{O}(\log_p^h)\), where \(h = \nu_p(U_p\text{-eig})\).

Let’s work with \(G := {\mathop{\mathrm{PGSp}}}_4 \cong {\mathop{\mathrm{SO}}}_5\) and its subgroup \(H = \mathop{\mathrm{SO}}(2)\).

Fourier coefficients of Siegel cups forms for \({\mathop{\mathrm{Sp}}}_4(\mathbb{Z})\). By adelization, you end up with Bessel periods for \(H\) inside \(G\). You can go back and forth very easily. The nice thing about these Bessel periods is that if you now use the refined Gan–Gross–Prasad period formula of Ichino–Ikeda type (a theorem in this case), it tells you that once you square these Bessel periods, you get central \(L\)-values. \(\Gamma = {\mathop{\mathrm{Sp}}}_4(\mathbb{Z})\), \(S_k(\Gamma)\): Siegel cusp forms of weight \(k\) for \(\Gamma\). \[F(\gamma z) = \det(c z + d)^k F(z) \quad \text{ for all } \gamma = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \\ \end{pmatrix} \in \Gamma.\] \[S_k(\Gamma) =(\text{Saito--Kurokawa space}) \oplus(\text{general type}).\] For all \(F \in S_k(\Gamma)\), we have \[F(Z) \sum_{S \in \Lambda_2} a(F, S) e^{2 \pi i \mathop{\mathrm{trace}}(S Z)},\] where \[\Lambda_2 = \left\{ \begin{pmatrix} a & b/2 \\ b/2 & c \\ \end{pmatrix} : a, b, c \in \mathbb{Z}, a > 0, b^2 - 4 a c < 0 \right\}.\] We have \[a(F, S) = a(F, A^t S A) \quad \text{ for all } A \in {\mathop{\mathrm{SL}}}_2(\mathbb{Z}).\] We write \[c(S) = \gcd(a,b,c), \quad \mathop{\mathrm{disc}}(S) = - 4 \det(S).\] We can look at the quotient \(\Lambda_2 / {\mathop{\mathrm{SL}}}_2(\mathbb{Z})\).

Let’s now turn to the automorphic perspective and discuss Bessel periods. Let \[T \hookrightarrow {\mathop{\mathrm{GSp}}}_4 , \quad T \cong K^\times, \quad T \subset {\mathop{\mathrm{GL}}}_2 \subset {\mathop{\mathrm{GSp}}}_4.\] There is another group \[N = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & & \ast & \ast \\ & 1 & \ast & \ast \\ & & 1 & \\ & & & 1 \\ \end{pmatrix} \subseteq {\mathop{\mathrm{GSp}}}_4.\] Given an automorphic form \(\phi\) on \({\mathop{\mathrm{GSp}}}_4\) (with trivial central character, as we always assume in today’s talk), and given a Hecke character \(\Lambda\) of \(K\) with trivial restriction to \(\mathbb{Q}\), i.e., \(\Lambda : \mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{Q} }^\times K^\times \backslash \mathbb{A}_K^\times \rightarrow \mathbb{C}^\times\). Then we consider \[B(\phi, \Lambda ) = \int_{[T N]} \phi(h) \Lambda(h) \psi_{S_d }(h) \, d h.\] Here \[\psi_T(x) := \psi(\mathop{\mathrm{trace}}(T x)),\] where \(\psi\) is a fixed nontrivial unitary character of \(\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{Q}} / \mathbb{Q}\), and \[S_d = \begin{pmatrix} - \frac{d}{4} & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \\ \end{pmatrix}.\]

So far, we’ve talked about classical Fourier coefficients and this classical quantity \(R(F, \Lambda)\). We have then introduced this purely automorphic object, the Bessel period \(B(\phi, \Lambda)\). The basic “adelization” fact (not deep) is the following. Let \(\Phi_F\) is the adelization of \(F \in S_k(\Gamma)\). Let \(D\) be a discriminant. Let \(\Lambda \in \widehat{H_D}\).

  1. If \(D = d\) is fundamental, then \[B(\Phi_F, \Lambda ) = \frac{2}{h_d} R(F, \Lambda) e^{- 2 \pi \mathop{\mathrm{Tr}}(S_d)}.\]

  2. In general, similar but shift \(\Phi_F\).

\[\sum_{T \in H_d } \lvert a(F, T) \rvert^2 = \frac{h_d}{4} e^{4 \pi i \mathop{\mathrm{trace}}(S_d)} \sum_{\Lambda \in \widehat{H_D }} \lvert B(\Phi_F, \Lambda ) \rvert^2.\]

Theorem 25 (Conjectured by Liu 2016, proved by Furusawa–Morimoto 2022). We have \[\frac{\left\lvert B(\Phi_F, \Lambda ) \right\rvert^2 }{ \left\langle F, F \right\rangle} = C \frac{L(\tfrac{1}{2}, \pi_F \times \operatorname{AI}(\Lambda))}{L(1, \pi, \mathop{\mathrm{Ad}}) L(1, \chi_d)} \prod J_v,\] where \(J_v = 1\) unless \(v \mid d\) or \(v = \infty\).

The upshot is that we now have nice formulas that relate central \(L\)-values and Fourier coefficients.

  1. We have (for a fundamental discriminant \(d\) – we always assume that a lowercase \(d\) is fundamental) \[\frac{\left\lvert \sum_{S \in H_d} a(F, S) \Lambda(S) \right\rvert^2 }{\left\langle F, F \right\rangle} = \frac{2^{- 4}(4 \pi )^{2 k + 1} \lvert d \rvert^{k - 1}}{\Gamma(2 k - 1)} \frac{L(\tfrac{1}{2}, \pi_F \times \operatorname{AI}(\Lambda))}{L(1, \pi, \mathop{\mathrm{Ad}})}.\]

  2. We have \[\sum_{ \substack{ S \in \Lambda_2 / {\mathop{\mathrm{SL}}}_2(\mathbb{Z}) \\ c(S) = L, \\ \mathop{\mathrm{disc}}(S) = D } } \lvert a(F, S) \rvert^2 \asymp \left\langle F, F \right\rangle \frac{(4 \pi )^{2 k }}{\Gamma(2 k - 1)} \left\lvert L^2 D \right\rvert^{k - \frac{3}{2} + \varepsilon} \sum_{\Lambda \in \widehat{H_D}} \frac{L(\tfrac{1}{2}, \pi_F \times \theta_\Lambda)}{L(1, \pi, \mathop{\mathrm{Ad}})}.\] Here \(L\) always denotes the finite part of the \(L\)-function, omitting archimedean factors.
